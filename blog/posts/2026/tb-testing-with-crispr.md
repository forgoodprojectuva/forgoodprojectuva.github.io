Researchers at Tulane University have developed a CRISPR-backed way to test for tuberculosis (TB) that bypasses many of the current limitations of cost and access that make screening difficult in low-income communities. Traditionally, TB is tested for in sputum, bacteria-rich mucus from the respiratory system whose collection requires specialized training and equipment. Sputum microscopy and other methods of analysis pose a barrier to medical care in low-income communities that causes a large number of TB cases to go either untreated or undetected despite the disease being treatable. With TB being the world’s deadliest communicable disease, expanding access to dependable, frugal ways of testing for it is vital in combatting the ailment’s spread.

The CRISPR technology, dubbed ActCRISPR-TB, allows for amplified examination of bacteria in less TB-rich samples, like stool or saliva. CRISPR-edited proteins in the tests bind to specific genomic sequences in the TB bacteria, activating “indicator” molecules to alert scientists of TB’s presence. This means that in samples where TB concentration would be too low to detect, the CRISPR tech can pronounce its presence through the indicator molecules, enabling identification. This both allows for testing via tongue-swab or stool collection–which are much less resource-intensive than traditional methods–and has also been shown to be even more reliable than the systems currently in place. Developing a “one-pot” test for TB akin to a COVID test to use this new CRISPR technology in analyzing a simple mouth swab allows for cheaper, more portable kits that can service low-resource communities where testing is otherwise unavailable or unserviceable. Testing is also faster, delivering results in under an hour, decreasing the cost in time of getting tested, further encouraging the proliferation of care.

While the test has yet to be approved by global health organizations or implemented at a wider scale, its promise in delivering low-cost innovation to TB testing would be transformative to communities around the world that continue to suffer from the effects of the highly-treatable bacterial infection.
